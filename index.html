Start Here

One round encourages selfish behavior. Repeated rounds create consequences. That shift is where cooperation can emerge.

What Players Choose

Cooperate means short-term restraint for mutual gain.

Defect means short-term advantage by exploiting trust.

Why Repetition Matters

If players meet again, betrayal can be punished and trust can be rewarded.

This is why long relationships behave differently from one-shot encounters.

Real-Life Examples (Simple)

Neighbors Sharing Water

Both save water: both benefit long term.

One cheats while the other cooperates: trust breaks quickly.

Trade Between Countries

Both keep fair tariffs: stable growth.

One side suddenly defects with sanctions/tariffs: retaliation cycle begins.

Election Norms

All parties respect rules: institutions stay strong.

Norm-breaking for short-term gain can trap everyone in mutual damage.

Core payoff logic: mutual cooperation beats mutual defection over time, even if defection can win briefly.

Meet the Agents

These are the personalities running in the background.

Q-learning Agent

Learns from experience and reward. Behavior changes over time.

Always Cooperate (AllC)

Always trusts. Works with good actors, loses to exploiters.

Always Defect (AllD)

Always betrays. Wins some short-term rounds, harms long-term ecosystems.

Tit For Tat (TFT)

Starts cooperative, then mirrors your last move.

Grim Trigger

Cooperates until one betrayal, then defects forever.

Copykitten

Forgives one mistake; retaliates after repeated betrayal.

Random

Unpredictable behavior used as a baseline.

What We Do in the Background

  1. Agents repeatedly choose cooperate or defect using the same payoff rules.
  2. Rewards are calculated after each round.
  3. Learning agents update Q-values and adapt behavior episode by episode.
  4. Your browser renders charts and stores run history locally for comparison.
